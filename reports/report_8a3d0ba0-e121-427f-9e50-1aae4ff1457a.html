Executive Summary

This BI analysis provides strategic oversight into our supplier ecosystem, crucial for monitoring risk and ensuring supply chain resilience across all business units. The primary objective is to enable proactive corrective action, safeguarding operations and maintaining business continuity by identifying and mitigating potential supply disruptions. The dashboard categorizes suppliers by risk (High, Medium, Low) and tracks key performance indicators to offer a holistic view of supplier health and performance. By integrating critical metrics such as total supplier count, risk classification distribution, average risk score, on-time delivery percentages, and quality ratings, the analysis offers a comprehensive view of the supplier landscape. This enables leadership to quickly pinpoint high-risk suppliers, especially those associated with significant purchase spend or declining performance. The ability to drill down by Business Unit and analyze trends over time provides invaluable context, facilitating targeted interventions to improve supplier reliability, address quality issues, and proactively manage potential supply chain vulnerabilities before they escalate into costly disruptions.

Recommendations

Here are 3-5 specific recommendations for implementation based on your BI analysis: 1. **Develop and Integrate a Granular Risk Scoring Framework:** While "Average Supplier Risk Score" is a KPI, for proactive action, the underlying *drivers* of risk need to be clearly defined and integrated into the BI system. Implement a detailed framework that categorizes risk factors (e.g., financial stability, geopolitical, operational resilience, quality history, delivery performance, single-source dependency, regulatory compliance) and assigns weighted scores. This allows the BI system to not just show *that* a supplier is high risk, but *why*, enabling targeted corrective actions. 2. **Establish Automated Alerts and Escalation Workflows:** To enable proactive corrective action, the BI system should not just display high-risk suppliers but actively notify relevant stakeholders. Configure automated alerts (e.g., email, system notifications) when a supplier's risk score crosses a predefined threshold, or when key performance indicators like On-Time Delivery or Quality Rating drop below acceptable levels. Integrate these alerts with predefined escalation paths to ensure timely review and assignment of corrective actions to specific teams or individuals. 3. **Integrate Supplier Risk with Product/Service Criticality and Purchase Spend:** While "Purchase Spend" is a KPI, the impact of a supply disruption isn't solely tied to spend; it's also about the criticality of the supplied item or service to your operations. Enhance the BI analysis to cross-reference high-risk suppliers with the criticality of the products/services they provide. This will allow the business to prioritize corrective actions on high-risk suppliers providing mission-critical components, thereby preventing the most impactful supply disruptions. 4. **Implement a Structured Feedback Loop for Supplier Performance Data:** Beyond automated metrics like On-Time Delivery and Quality Rating (which might be system-generated), establish a process for capturing qualitative and quantitative feedback from various internal stakeholders (e.g., receiving, production, engineering, finance, legal) regarding supplier interactions, audit results, non-conformance reports, and contract compliance. This ensures a comprehensive and holistic view of supplier performance is continuously fed into the risk scoring model, enriching the BI insights and making risk assessments more accurate and actionable.

Data Sources & Preparation The dashboard relies on data from multiple enterprise systems. An Extract, Transform, Load (ETL) or Extract, Load, Transform (ELT) process will be required to consolidate and prepare the data, ideally within a data warehouse or data lakehouse environment. ### A. Core Data Entities 1. **Suppliers Master Data** * **Source:** ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning), SRM (Supplier Relationship Management) system, or dedicated Supplier Master Data Management (MDM) system. * **Key Fields:** * `SupplierID` (Primary Key, unique identifier) * `SupplierName` * `BusinessUnitID` (Foreign Key to Business Units) * `RiskClassification` (e.g., 'High', 'Medium', 'Low') - *This might be derived or directly stored.* * `CurrentRiskScore` (Numerical score) * `LastRiskAssessmentDate` * `SupplierStatus` (e.g., 'Active', 'Inactive') 2. **Purchase Orders & Deliveries Data** * **Source:** ERP, Procurement System. * **Key Fields:** * `OrderID` (Primary Key) * `SupplierID` (Foreign Key) * `OrderDate` * `RequestedDeliveryDate` (or `DueDate`) * `ActualDeliveryDate` * `LineItemValue` (Monetary spend for each item/line) * `Currency` * `QuantityOrdered` * `QuantityReceived` 3. **Supplier Quality Data** * **Source:** Quality Management System (QMS), manual assessment forms, or integrated ERP quality modules. * **Key Fields:** * `QualityRecordID` (Primary Key) * `SupplierID` (Foreign Key) * `QualityScore` (Numerical rating, e.g., 1-100) * `QualityRating` (Categorical, e.g., 'Excellent', 'Good', 'Fair', 'Poor') - *Can be derived from `QualityScore`.* * `AssessmentDate` * `DefectCount` * `IssueType` 4. **Business Units Master Data (Optional, if separate table)** * **Source:** ERP, MDM. * **Key Fields:** * `BusinessUnitID` (Primary Key) * `BusinessUnitName` ### B. Data Transformation & Logic An ETL/ELT pipeline will perform the following steps: 1. **Data Extraction:** Pull raw data from source systems. 2. **Data Cleaning:** Handle nulls, duplicates, and inconsistencies (e.g., standardizing supplier names, date formats). 3. **Data Integration/Joining:** * Join `Suppliers Master Data` with `Business Units Master Data` on `BusinessUnitID`. * Join `Purchase Orders & Deliveries Data` with `Suppliers Master Data` on `SupplierID`. * Join `Supplier Quality Data` with `Suppliers Master Data` on `SupplierID`. 4. **Dimension Creation:** * **Time Dimension:** Create a robust `Date Dimension` table (if not already present) that includes `Date`, `Year`, `Quarter`, `Month`, `Week`, `DayOfWeek`, `IsWeekend`, etc. This will be used to filter and aggregate time-based measures. * **Risk Classification:** Ensure this is consistently categorized (High, Medium, Low) either directly from the source or derived based on `CurrentRiskScore` thresholds. 5. **Measure Calculation Logic:** * **Total Count of Suppliers:** * `COUNT(DISTINCT SupplierID)` from `Suppliers Master Data` (filtered for active suppliers). * **Count of High/Medium/Low Risk Suppliers:** * `COUNT(DISTINCT SupplierID)` from `Suppliers Master Data` where `RiskClassification` matches the category. * **Average Risk Score:** * `AVG(CurrentRiskScore)` from `Suppliers Master Data`. * **Percentage of On-Time Deliveries:** * Calculate `IsOnTime` flag: `CASE WHEN ActualDeliveryDate <= RequestedDeliveryDate THEN 1 ELSE 0 END`. * `SUM(IsOnTime) / COUNT(OrderID)` from `Purchase Orders & Deliveries Data`. * *Considerations:* Define "on-time" precisely (e.g., exactly on date, within X days tolerance). Handle cases where `ActualDeliveryDate` is null. * **Supplier Quality Score/Rating:** * `AVG(QualityScore)` from `Supplier Quality Data` (for a numerical average). * Alternatively, for rating, use the `QualityRating` directly or derive it from the average score over a period. * *Aggregation Strategy:* Decide if this is the latest score, average over a period (e.g., last 12 months), or a weighted average. * **Total Monetary Spend:** * `SUM(LineItemValue)` from `Purchase Orders & Deliveries Data`. * *Considerations:* Implement currency conversion if transactions occur in multiple currencies (e.g., convert all to USD using daily exchange rates). --- ## II. Risks & Assumptions

Here are potential risks and assumptions for the BI initiative: ## Potential Risks: 1. **Data Inconsistency and Quality Issues:** The KPIs (especially Quality Rating, On-Time Delivery Percentage, and even Supplier Risk Score definition) may be captured differently or with varying levels of accuracy across different business units and source systems. This could lead to an unreliable "average supplier risk score" and inaccurate risk classifications, eroding trust in the BI solution. 2. **Lack of Standardized Supplier Risk Definition and Scoring Methodology:** The initiative relies heavily on "High/Medium/Low Risk Suppliers" and "Average Supplier Risk Score." If there isn't a universally agreed-upon, objective, and quantifiable methodology to define and calculate these scores across all business units, the classifications will be subjective, inconsistent, and not comparable, making proactive action difficult. 3. **Lack of Clear Actionable Insights and Defined Processes for Corrective Action:** The objective is to "enable proactive corrective action." If the BI dashboard only *identifies* risks without providing context for *why* a supplier is high risk, or if there aren't clear organizational processes, ownership, and resources allocated for *acting* on these insights, the initiative will fail to achieve its core objective of preventing supply disruptions. 4. **Low User Adoption and Resistance to Change:** Business units or procurement teams might be resistant to using a new centralized tool, especially if they have existing (even if less effective) methods. If the data is not trusted, the interface is not intuitive, or the value proposition isn't clearly demonstrated, users may not engage with the dashboard, rendering it ineffective. ## Potential Assumptions: 1. **Availability and Accessibility of Comprehensive, Accurate Source Data:** It is assumed that all necessary data for the KPIs (On-Time Delivery, Quality Rating, Purchase Spend, and underlying data for Supplier Risk Score) is readily available, consistently captured, and accessible from existing systems (e.g., ERP, SRM, quality management systems) across all business units. 2. **Organizational Buy-in and Commitment to Act on Insights:** It is assumed that there is strong executive sponsorship and organizational will to not only monitor supplier risk but also to invest resources and implement changes based on the insights provided by the BI dashboard. This includes allocating budget and personnel for corrective actions. 3. **Standardized Definition of Supplier Risk and Data Elements Can Be Established/Agreed Upon:** It is assumed that a common, objective, and quantifiable definition of "supplier risk" can be established and agreed upon by all relevant stakeholders and business units. This also assumes that the underlying data elements (e.g., what constitutes "on-time delivery" or "quality issue") are consistently defined or can be harmonized.
